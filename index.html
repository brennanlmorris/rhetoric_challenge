Suggestions for impromptu or short-format speaking

These guidelines are relevant when needing to give short statements, such as in media interviews, "elevator pitches", and quick reaction.

Principles to consider

  • Lead with the lede: State your main idea or argument upfront—don't bury the lede or your best material in the middle. What's the one thing you want people to take away—just say it.
  • Control the frame: Frame / define key terms and concepts in ways that support your position (though within reason and usually subtly).
  • Tell stories: Use concrete examples and relatable stories rather than abstract concepts—people remember narratives.
  • Objection handling: Quickly recognize obvious counter-arguments, then refute or explain why they don't undermine your position.
  • Start on common ground: If possible, lean into what your audience already accepts before introducing more controversial claims. How are you giving people what they care about?
  • "So What?": Show why your argument matters to your audience's lives or values.

Delivery notes

  • Project confidence from word one: Start with conviction even if you're still formulating your argument—strong openings create momentum.
  • Slow but energetic: Generally speak more deliberately than feels comfortable. But convey energy through posture, projection, and expression.
  • Embrace strategic pauses: When you need thinking time, pause confidently rather than rushing—silence feels longer to you than to listeners.
  • Don't overthink.

Elements to consider including

  • 1-2 key supporting points (keep it simple)
  • Concrete, relatable examples (from everyday life, history, or shared experience)
  • Brief acknowledgment of counter-arguments (show you understand the complexity)
  • Clear relevance statement (why this matters to your audience)
